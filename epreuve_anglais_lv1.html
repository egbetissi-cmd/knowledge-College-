Context

La langue anglaise est considérée, de nos jours, comme celle de la mondialisation par excellence. C'est pourquoi elle prend de plus en plus le dessus sur les autres langues en Afrique. Ici, tu auras à réagir par rapport à la montée fulgurante de cette langue, même dans les pays dits francophones.

English on the Rise in Africa
  1. Today, 26 countries in Sub-Saharan Africa use English either as an official language exclusively (like Nigeria and Ghana) or as an official language alongside another African language (like in Kenya or South Africa). Several of those countries are traditionally Francophone nations (like Cameroon and Seychelles). However, English is used for communicative purposes in some 53 countries in Africa.
  2. Only nine percent of the global population is native English speakers, but almost one third of the population speaks English. Some countries have more than one official language. Africa currently has at least 2,400 languages. Many of these languages are local, tribal languages with limited utility beyond their local region. Of these estimated languages, only 50 have more than 500,000 speakers and only 10 have more than one million speakers. Key regional Sub-Saharan African languages include Afrikaans and Xhosa in the South; Fulani; Yoruba; Hausa and Igbo in the West; Amharic and Oromo in the East; and Kiswahili in the East.
  3. While researchers continue to emphasize the increasing role of English in Africa, it is also notable that French is on the decline. While the decline of the French language can be seen throughout the Francophone world, the most striking examples are found in Africa: "South of the Sahara", countries which formerly had large French-speaking populations are making the switch to English due to its relevance in Southern Africa, as well as internationally. English, meanwhile, is becoming the most important Western language in Africa. It has been argued that in two decades, French may not be spoken in Africa at all.
  4. English is used as a medium of education in many African countries, from primary school up to the tertiary level. In the former French colonies of Ivory Coast, Mali and Senegal, English is the first compulsory foreign language taught. While courses at tertiary institutions have, in many countries, been taught in English for some time, countries are now recognizing the value of introducing English language learning in the early primary years.
  5. Francophone countries seeking economic development often turn to English as a means to engage foreign markets. In order to attract foreign investments, develop international trade opportunities and create tourism campaigns, countries in Africa often see English as an essential tool. As African economies look to increase their commodity exports, the security of millions of African farmers depends on the negotiation and marketing skills — including their command of English — possessed by the leaders and exporters who represent them.
Patrick Plonski, African Studies Association Annual Conference, November 23, 2013, Baltimore, Maryland.

Item 1 — Right or Wrong (5 pts)

  • 1. Wrong — Nigeria and Ghana use English exclusively as official language, not alongside another African language (§1).
  • 2. Wrong — Only 50 African languages have more than 500,000 speakers, not fifteen (§2).
  • 3. Right — "countries which formerly had large French-speaking populations are making the switch to English" (§3).
  • 4. Wrong — English is the first compulsory (not optional) foreign language taught (§4).
  • 5. Right — "countries in Africa often see English as an essential tool" for business and exports (§5).

Item 2 — Questions (4 pts)

  • 1. Only nine percent of the world's population has English as their mother tongue.
  • 2. Africa currently has at least 2,400 local languages in Sub-Saharan Africa.
  • 3. French may not be spoken in Africa at all in twenty years / French could disappear from Africa.
  • 4. Two reasons: (i) to attract foreign investments and develop international trade; (ii) to create tourism campaigns and engage foreign markets.

Item 3 — Synonyms (5 pts)

  • 1. "At this time" → currently (§2)
  • 2. "Accentuate" → emphasize (§3)

  • 4. "Overseas" → international (§5)
  • 5. "Instrument" → tool (§5)

Part B — Model Speech

Model Speech

Good morning/afternoon, distinguished guests, teachers, and fellow students,

It is my great honour to stand before you today on this English Speaker's Day to share my thoughts on the importance of the English language in our lives and in our future.

First and foremost, English is the global language of communication. With over one billion speakers worldwide, it connects people across cultures, nations and continents. Whether you are doing business, travelling, or pursuing higher education, English opens doors that would otherwise remain closed.

Secondly, English is the language of opportunity. As we have seen in our own continent, African countries are increasingly adopting English to attract foreign investment and grow their economies. Mastering English gives us, the youth of francophone Africa, a competitive advantage in the international job market.

Furthermore, English is the language of science, technology, and innovation. Most of the world's scientific research, technological advances, and digital content are produced in English. Without English, we risk being left behind in an ever-changing world.

In conclusion, English is not a threat to our identity — it is a bridge to the world. Let us embrace it with pride and determination. Thank you.
